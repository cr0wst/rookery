The first firefly of the season—if you see one at all anymore—carries news. A decade ago, you might have caught a dozen in a single evening, blinking in the tall grass behind the house. Now you look and look, stepping through dusk with the kind of attention children give the dark, and the light you find is only what we ourselves have made.

Fireflies are leaving. Over the past several decades, populations have collapsed across North America—not everywhere at once, but steadily, in the kind of slow fade that goes unnoticed until you realize a whole summer feeling has vanished. Scientists point to two culprits, both born of the way we've remade the landscape: light pollution and habitat loss. Together, they've turned what should be June's small miracle into a rarity in many places.

The mechanics are straightforward and sobering. [Fireflies use their flash—that amber bioluminescence that defines summer—for mating, a language they've spoken in darkness for millions of years.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Firefly) When the night sky glows with artificial light, even the glow of distant streetlamps and house lights, that ancient conversation fails. The beetles can't find each other. A female flashing her signal on a lawn can't be seen by males searching overhead. Light drowns them out not with malice but with noise. 

Meanwhile, the tall grass, the wetlands, and the unmowed edges where firefly larvae develop are disappearing under lawns, parking lots, and manicured fields. The larvae spend months in soil or low vegetation, developing in the dark. Without the wild margins, without the forgotten corners our gardens once left alone, they simply cannot complete their life.

The decline matters not just for what it costs us—that summer you'll never quite get back, the kind of easy enchantment children used to know without thinking. It matters because fireflies are sensitive indicators. A landscape that's lost its fireflies has lost its wild edges, its dark nights, its unmapped space. When the light goes out in June, it's telling you something about what we've done to the places we live.

There's a peculiar cruelty in how this happens. It's not dramatic enough to prompt the kind of alarm that dying eagles once did. You don't notice it the way you'd notice an absence—you just find yourself searching harder each year, staying out later, driving farther to the darker places on the map. By the time you realize they're truly gone, the machinery of their disappearance has been running for decades.

Some states and cities have begun to notice. A few towns have dimmed lights in summer months. Some people have stopped mowing their lawns so aggressively, letting the grass grow wild. It's small, incremental work, but it's work. The fireflies haven't left everywhere yet. They're still blinking in certain pockets of the country—places where the night is still dark enough, where the grass is still wild enough. If you want to see them, you'll want to know where those places are, and you'll want to go soon. That amber light is fading faster than most of us realized.
