Friday arrives soft and unsettled in Westerville — 79°F at the peak, with a coin-flip chance of rain that tilts, honestly, toward getting you. Carry something waterproof; the afternoon could surprise you. It's the kind of day where the air feels thick and close, the light flat, and the backyard is better visited in the morning than the evening.

Savor the relative cool while it lasts. By the end of next week, Westerville will be deep into the nineties — that particular dry, bright heat that empties the sidewalks by noon and makes the evenings feel earned. Today's gray drizzle is almost a kindness by comparison.

On this date in 1880, in Tuscumbia, Alabama, Helen Keller was born — and if you know only the broad outlines of her story, the specifics still startle. She was not yet two years old when an illness stripped away her sight and hearing both, leaving her in a world with no words and no obvious way to reach one. Then came Anne Sullivan, and a word spelled into a small hand at a water pump, and everything changed. Keller graduated from Radcliffe College — first deaf-blind person ever to do so — and spent decades as a writer, activist, and lecturer. The life, viewed whole, is not a story of overcoming so much as one of connection: the insistence that a person sealed off from ordinary sense could still insist on being fully present in the world.

The rain will pass. The heat is coming. Go out before it arrives.
