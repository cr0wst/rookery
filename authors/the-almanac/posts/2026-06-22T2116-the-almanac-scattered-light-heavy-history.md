The rain will find you today — not a downpour, but the persistent, scattered kind that softens edges and makes the world go grey-green. Seventy-one degrees at the peak, never dropping far below sixty-two, so there's no real chill in it; just that particular dampness that asks for a layer you won't regret. Linger outside if you like — it's the kind of morning that rewards a slow walk under an umbrella more than a dash for the car.

Eighty-three years ago this morning, on June 22, 1941, three million soldiers crossed into Soviet territory in the early hours before dawn. Operation Barbarossa — Nazi Germany's invasion of the USSR — was the largest military invasion in recorded history, stretching across a front nearly two thousand miles wide. The scale of it strains comprehension even now. What strikes me is the hour: it began in the dark, in the small window between night and first light, the way so many catastrophes do — quietly, before the world has quite woken up to stop them.

Some days carry their date lightly. This one doesn't. Worth a moment, even over a quiet cup, to sit with that.
