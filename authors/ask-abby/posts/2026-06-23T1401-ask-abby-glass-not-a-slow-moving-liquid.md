You've probably heard it: glass is actually a liquid, just moving so slowly we can't tell. The proof, people say, is those old church windows — thicker at the bottom, thin at the top — glass that has oozed downward over the centuries. It's a tidy little story. It's also wrong, and it's been wrong for a long time.

**Glass is a solid.** More specifically, it's what materials scientists call an *amorphous solid* — a [solid whose atoms are arranged without the long-range periodic order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amorphous_solid) that defines a crystal. The atoms are locked in place, unable to flow past one another. That's what makes it rigid at room temperature, and that's what makes it a solid by any reasonable definition.

The confusion arises from two things: a quirk of chemistry and a quirk of history.

**The chemistry quirk.** When molten glass cools, it doesn't snap into a crystal lattice the way water freezes into ice. Instead it passes through what's called the *glass transition* — a gradual stiffening, a viscosity that climbs through astronomical values until the material is, for every practical purpose, a rigid solid. Because this transition is continuous rather than sharp, glass occupies an odd conceptual no-man's-land. Some physicists once described it informally as a "supercooled liquid," meaning its atomic arrangement resembles a frozen snapshot of the liquid state. But [informal description is not physical classification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glass): at room temperature, glass has a viscosity estimated at around 10²⁰ pascal-seconds — roughly a trillion trillion times that of water. For detectable flow on human timescales, you'd need to wait far longer than the age of the universe. It does not flow. It is a solid.

**The window quirk.** So why are old cathedral windows thicker at the bottom? The answer is [crown glass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crown_glass_(window)), the dominant window-making technique for much of medieval and early-modern Europe. Glassblowers would spin a blown globe of molten glass into a flat disc — a process that left the disc with a natural variation in thickness: thinner toward the edge, thicker near the centre (where the pontil rod attached, leaving the distinctive "bull's-eye" knot). When glaziers cut panes from these discs, they had uneven material to work with. Thick edges placed at the bottom simply made practical sense: a heavier edge at the bottom keeps a pane more stable in its frame, and a glazier with a sensible eye for gravity would set it that way. Not all old windows follow this pattern, by the way — survey enough medieval churches and you'll find thick edges at the top, the sides, and occasionally in no consistent direction at all. The "always thicker at the bottom" claim doesn't even survive contact with the data.

What glass actually is, then, is genuinely strange without needing any myth to dress it up. It's a material with no melting point in the conventional sense, no crystal structure, and a transition to rigidity so gradual that chemists spent decades arguing about how to classify it. "Amorphous solid" is the honest answer — not liquid, not crystal, something in between that nature has been making (in the form of obsidian and fulgurite) long before humans ever figured out how.

The liquid myth persists, I suspect, because it flatters our intuition: matter flows, given enough time. Glass politely declines to cooperate.
