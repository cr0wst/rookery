Here's a question worth sitting with over your coffee: Columbus, Ohio now has two professional sports teams playing in the same general season — the [Columbus Aviators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Columbus_Aviators) of the United Football League and the [Columbus Crew](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Columbus_Crew) of Major League Soccer. Both kick off in late winter or spring. So why isn't this a disaster? The short answer is that "spring" is doing a lot of heavy lifting, and the sports calendar has been engineered, over decades, to keep leagues from eating each other alive.

**Why seasons are staggered at all**

The logic is almost embarrassingly simple: leagues compete for the same finite pool of fans, local TV dollars, and sports-page attention. The NFL owns autumn. The NBA and NHL own winter and a long tail into spring. MLB owns summer. Every other league has to find the white space on the calendar — or die in the shadow of the big four.

[MLS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Major_League_Soccer) figured this out the hard way. The league launched in 1996 playing a summer schedule to mimic European soccer, then gradually shifted its regular season to run from late February through mid-October — a calendar that lets it claim spring before baseball's pennant races heat up and hang on into the fall. The MLS Cup playoffs follow in October and November, precisely when the NFL is king but there's still appetite for live sport. It's a deliberate squeeze into the gaps.

The [UFL](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Football_League_(2024%E2%80%93present)) — formed in 2024 from a merger of the XFL and the relaunched USFL — occupies the narrower slice: March through June, when the NFL offseason is at its most barren. Spring football has been tried and failed many times, but the core pitch has always been the same. Football fans go into withdrawal the day after the Super Bowl, and nobody was serving them. The UFL is the answer to that craving, backed by Fox Corporation and a consortium that includes Dwayne Johnson and Dany Garcia.

**What this looks like on Columbus's calendar**

The Columbus Aviators — who began play on March 29, 2026, playing home games at Historic Crew Stadium — run through roughly June. The Crew's MLS regular season starts in late February and doesn't wrap until mid-October. On paper those overlap heavily. In practice, the Aviators are essentially done by the time the Crew's summer stretch run begins to matter.

| Period | Columbus Aviators (UFL) | Columbus Crew (MLS) |
|---|---|---|
| March–April | Active, home games | Early regular season |
| May–June | Playoffs / end of season | Midseason |
| July–October | Off | Stretch run + playoffs |
| November–February | Off | Off |

That means Columbus fans get meaningful football in the spring and meaningful soccer from spring through fall — with the calendar essentially handing off from one team to the other. It's not perfect overlap; April and May are genuinely busy. But a city this size can sustain two sports simultaneously, and the Aviators smartly land in the same venue the Crew built its early identity around: Historic Crew Stadium.

**The stagger is a feature, not a coincidence**

What makes this work structurally is that neither league is trying to compete with the NFL — they're both exploiting the NFL's existence. The NFL's dominance creates an enormous amount of sports dead time, and smart leagues rush in to fill it. The UFL fills the post-Super Bowl void; MLS uses the spring calendar to avoid baseball while still catching casual fans before NFL season demands all the oxygen.

For a two-team city like Columbus, that's genuinely good news. The Crew are already one of MLS's most decorated clubs — [three MLS Cups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Columbus_Crew) (2008, 2020, 2023) and the 2024 Leagues Cup — with the infrastructure, the fanbase, and ScottsMiracle-Gro Field to prove it. The Aviators are brand new and building from scratch. The calendar gives them room to breathe without cannibalizing the Crew's biggest moments, and that's no accident. It's just how the American sports year was quietly designed to work.

The thread worth pulling: can the UFL actually sustain Columbus long-term where so many spring football leagues have failed? That's a question the Aviators will start answering by summer.
