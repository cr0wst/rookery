When Robert Baden-Powell founded the Boy Scouts in 1908, he was explicit about what he wanted: young men trained in outdoor skills, self-reliance, and military preparation. When Girl Guides launched a few years later—[starting informally around 1910 and formalizing by 1914](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Girl_Guides)—it wasn't an accidental afterthought. It was a deliberate response to girls who wanted in, shaped by Baden-Powell and his sister Agnes, and designed from the ground up as something fundamentally different.

The early ideology couldn't have been starker. [Boy Scouts emphasized camping, navigation, knot-tying, and field skills](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scouting_America)—the practical mastery of wilderness and terrain. Girl Guides, by contrast, were organized around domestic competence, health, and service to the community. Where boys learned to survive outdoors, girls learned to care for others: first aid, cooking, childcare, needlework. The curriculum wasn't incomplete Boy Scouting; it was *intentionally different*, rooted in the assumption that girls and boys had separate social roles to fill.

The organizations reflected the gendered anxieties of early 20th-century Britain. Boys needed hardening and preparation for empire and military life. Girls needed refining and training for motherhood and household management—even if, as in the Girl Guides' case, that extended to community and voluntary service. Girl Guides wore uniforms and earned badges, just as scouts did, but the badges told you the philosophy: "Entertainer," "Child Nurse," "Needlewoman," not "Navigator" or "Camper."

For nearly a century, that split held. Girl Guides and Girl Scouts (in America) remained parallel universes—similar in structure and spirit, wholly separate in expectation. Boys got the outdoors and adventure. Girls got the home and service. It was efficient and tidy, and it worked, right up until it didn't.

The real shift began in earnest in the 1970s and 80s, when youth culture and feminism started colliding with scouting tradition. [By the 1990s and 2000s, both Girl Guides and Girl Scouts began offering outdoor skills, camping, and adventure activities that were once Boy Scout preserves.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Girl_Guides) The uniforms changed. The badge systems expanded. Girl Guides started training in survival skills. Girl Scouts added rock climbing and wilderness expeditions to their programs.

Today, the ideological distance has compressed enormously. Modern Girl Guides and Girl Scouts offer the same outdoor competencies, the same technical training, and the same emphasis on leadership and self-reliance that their male counterparts do. Many organizations now run mixed-gender troops. The home economics and childcare focus hasn't vanished—caregiving is still taught, and service remains central—but it's no longer the whole story, and it's no longer gendered. Boys and girls alike can choose to learn it.

What's interesting is how recently this change really took hold. For sixty or seventy years, the gender split was so normalized that few questioned it. The movements evolved, but along carefully segregated tracks. It took the cultural weight of feminism, changing ideas about what girls could and should do, and a whole generation of young women who simply wanted the same opportunities as the boys down the street, to crack the original design.

The gendered foundations are still visible if you know where to look—Girl Guides still emphasize community service and leadership in the round, while some Boy Scout programs lean slightly harder into outdoor skills and competition. But the wall between them is much lower now. Whether that's a loss of tradition or a gain in fairness depends partly on who you ask, and partly on what you think scouting is *for*. But the ideology that sent boys out to conquer mountains and girls into the kitchen to prepare for service? That one's unmistakably done.
