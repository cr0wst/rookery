The short answer to the second half of Steve's question: no, "rookery" does not come from crows. It comes from the *rook* — *Corvus frugilegus*, a closely related but distinct European corvid. Rooks nest collectively in the tops of tall trees, often near farms and villages, and those noisy treetop colonies are called rookeries. The word dates to around 1725, built simply from "rook" + "-ery," and it stuck so well that it migrated to describe any dense breeding colony — penguins, seals, herons — long after anyone stopped thinking about the bird.

The crow gathering at dusk is something different: a communal *roost*, not a rookery. And it's one of the more striking things birds do.

**Why do they do it?**

Ornithologists point to three interlocking reasons:

| Reason | What it means in practice |
|---|---|
| **Safety in numbers** | A predator — say, a Great Horned Owl — finds it far harder to single out one bird from a mob of ten thousand than from a lone pair in a tree. |
| **Warmth** | Packed bodies in a sheltered stand of trees hold heat through cold nights. Winter roosts in particular can swell into the hundreds of thousands for this reason. |
| **Information sharing** | This one is the most interesting. Crows that failed to find food can follow successful foragers out of the roost at dawn, effectively using the gathering as a nightly bulletin board for where to eat. |

Communal roosting responds to environmental signals — nightfall most obviously — and the birds return to the same site night after night. Roost sizes range from a few hundred to well over a million individuals in large winter aggregations.

There's also a social dimension that researchers are still piecing together. Crows are highly intelligent, long-memoried birds, and roosts appear to serve as places where family groups reunite and where young birds meet potential mates. It's less a parking lot and more a town square.

One thread worth pulling later: why do crow roosts tend to shift toward city centers in winter? Urban heat islands, reduced owl pressure, and bright lights (which deter owl attacks) all seem to play a role — but the relative weight of each is still debated.
