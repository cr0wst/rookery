When I read a news story now, I want one simple thing: honesty about what I'm reading. If a machine wrote it, *tell me*. And while you're at it, show me the work.

Here's the thing about trustworthiness — it's not about the technology. It's about *transparency*. People don't distrust AI because it's AI; we distrust anything that looks hand-written but wasn't, anything that hides its seams.

## Disclosure First

The easiest and most important rule: label it. If a story was written or significantly composed by a language model or automated system, that needs to be visible — in the byline, the editor's note, somewhere a reader encounters it before deciding whether to click. That's not because AI stories are inherently worse. It's because I need to know what I'm dealing with. Am I reading a summary? A draft someone refined? A fully autonomous piece? The answer changes what I'm evaluating.

This matters because readers have trust *equations*. I might trust Reuters' automated earnings reports because I know Reuters' process and because earnings data is structured and verifiable. I'd be warier of an AI-written political analysis with no human editorial gatekeeping — not because the words are robot-made, but because I can't see who's responsible for the judgment calls. Transparency is how you build that trust.

## Show Your Sources

The second demand is even more basic: citations. [Scientists exploring GPT-4](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-023-00816-5) noted frustration with how AI systems hide their reasoning — the underlying engineering is "cloaked in secrecy." That secrecy erodes trust. Readers face the same problem with AI-written news: *where did you get that fact? How did you choose which angle mattered?*

If a machine wrote the piece, every factual claim should come with a direct line to its source — not a vague reference, but an actual link the reader can follow. This is harder than it sounds: large language models can sound authoritative while fabricating or confusing details. An AI-written story *must* carry receipts. Your source links become the thing a reader can verify when the prose itself is opaque.

## Show the Process

The third — and most interesting — piece is showing the work. What did a human contribute? Where did the machine take over? If an editor rewrote a paragraph, did that shift the meaning? If the AI summarized ten sources into one sentence, what got lost?

There's actually an audience for this. [Public interest in understanding AI's role in news](https://www.npr.org/sections/technology/) has grown steadily. People *want* to know. They want to see the joins. And when the joins are visible — when you can see "reporter filed the original reporting, AI synthesized the timeline, editor fact-checked the numbers" — that often *increases* trust rather than eroding it. It shows the work, the checkpoints, the accountability.

## What Doesn't Matter (or Shouldn't)

For the record: it's not about style or readability. AI-written prose can be clear and well-paced. It's not about whether a story is "important enough" for human writers — I'll take a well-sourced AI-drafted earnings summary over a hand-written but sloppy one. The technology itself isn't the problem.

The problem is the same one journalism has always faced: *who decided what mattered, and how do I know they did their job?* With a human reporter, I can see their byline and track record. With a machine, I need something else — disclosure, sources I can check, and visibility into the process. That's not anti-AI. It's pro-reader.
